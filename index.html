Chapter One

The Beginning

I often wonder how my life would have turned out had I not lost my innocence in a world that was insane. My name is Luke Arrow Ferguson — and this is my story, to the best of my knowledge and memory, and as told by some of my family.

Where to begin — it's hard to think as my mind races with thoughts of my past. Searching deep in my psyche and the wall that's been built of memories long ago. Memories that should never come out, and events that are too hard to fathom, much less contemplate.

I guess you could say that I was born in war and baptized in blood. In either case, it made me the man I am today. But with that said, it set the stage for who I would become in later years — the man who earned the name Warpath by his brothers in one of the most dangerous prison gangs in the system.

"It is my hope and passion that whoever is reading this — it helps you in some sense of the word, knowing that you are not alone. And whatever you're going through in life, you can make a difference in someone else's."

After all, if I can make a difference in helping someone better themselves and learn from my mistakes, then what I went through in life was worth it — it was God's Design. Kind of like Saul in the Bible who became Paul.

From what I can remember in my early, early years — I think I was about maybe three or four — through those eyes, the eyes of a loving grandmother who loved me. But what I saw was blank, cold, and nothing there. I could not understand, much less contemplate, what was going on. But grandma was dead.

It's still disputed to this day that grandfather killed her in a fit of rage and drunkenness. But I don't remember — I was too young, or my mind shut down. I don't really know for sure. All I remember of my early years are really bits and pieces. For some reason, my mind started to build a wall — a way to protect me — is the only way I can describe it.

Keep in mind: most of what I'm describing comes from relatives and bits and pieces of memories.

How I came to live with my grandparents is also disputed. My mother had me at age 16. Some say grandpa took me from the hospital, but I don't quite know. I was raised with my uncles and aunts — as one once put it — as the little brother, since my mom was the second child to be born out of eight children: four boys and four girls.

I don't know much about my grandmother's side. But what I do know is this — she was Lakota Sioux Indian, and I come from a long line of warriors. So I am Sioux Indian and Scandinavian descent — which would make me a Viking Indian, I guess you could say.

Which is kind of funny in a way. I never knew the white side of that genealogy until the year 2017 — or much less who my father was until then — which I was 46 years old and in prison at the time.

His Blood, His Bones

Two Warrior Bloodlines, One Unbreakable Man

🦅

Lakota Sioux

The Lakota are among the most renowned warrior peoples of the Great Plains — fierce, spiritual, and deeply connected to the land. Their tradition of bravery, endurance, and communal loyalty runs through Luke's veins. From his grandmother's bloodline, he inherits the spirit of warriors who never surrendered.

⚡

Scandinavian Viking

The Norse Vikings were legendary seafarers, explorers, and fighters — known for their resilience against impossible odds, their code of honor, and their belief in destiny. Luke's Scandinavian heritage gives him the blood of men who carved their names into history through sheer force of will.

Two of the world's greatest warrior traditions — the Plains warrior and the Norse berserker — merged in a single human being. It is no accident that Luke Arrow Ferguson became who he is. His blood demanded it.

The Story Continues

Surviving the Storm

The year was 1973, and frost was still in the air. I remember waking up and hearing gunfire. But at the time I could not quite vaguely understand what was going on, as people were running and screaming everywhere. I often to this day hear those screams — but before I can get a picture of that thought, the memory's gone, like a fart in the wind, and fades from memory, and the screams fade with it.

Growing up for me was not ideal, to say the least. My life from 1973, after grandmother's death, was extremely painful — and set the stage for the beginning of my life and who I would become in later years. Because to this day I can often still hear grandfather's voice at times, echoing from times past when my anger flares.

I think somewhere around the age of five or so — can't really remember — I started hearing the voices. But more like whispers, you could say. Until I started getting older. Grandfather — one might put it — I don't think quite understood. He just whipped me a lot. Grandpa, back in those days, was feared by a lot of people. He was a man that was respected and demanded respect, or you got your ass handed to you if not killed. He was a WWII vet that saw a lot of action and more or less killed a lot of people. In a sense, he was all messed up. Maybe that's why he was a drunk — but also meaner than a rattlesnake and just as fast.

"I remember one time — it was my first day of school. I was followed by two kids on my way home. Somewhere between there they made their move, and the fight was on..."

I lost that fight. While I was lying on the ground, bloody, battered, and beaten, they walked away laughing. Ashamed of my loss, I managed to get up and limped home. I can tell you — that was one of the longest walks I ever did.

But what awaited was far worse than what I had gotten. You see, grandpa hated weakness — hated it with every fiber of his soul, and that's speaking it lightly. Upon my arrival, he'd already gotten word of my loss. He was waiting on the porch. When he saw me limping and battered, he calmly walked down and stood over me. I can tell you one thing — I didn't even see the punch coming as my head snapped back.

One sentence he said to me: "Get your ass beat again and I'll beat you harder." I don't know what I thought, but from there on I fought a lot that year — and never lost again.

Grandpa liked to travel a lot that year and the years that followed. He took me everywhere, and he did things to people that leave no room for imagination. One time I awoke in the car we had, and he was burning something out in the middle of nowhere. He said he hit a deer — but what he had wrapped up in some kind of blanket, I saw a foot as he was putting it in the hole.

It's so weird thinking about it now, kind of like yesterday, as that memory comes to mind. I keep telling people: stop poking holes in the dam of my memories. But I gave my word on writing this book, and as much as I feel uncomfortable — I am a man of my word.

The year I think was somewhere around 1975, give or take. I was always alone. I remember when I started hearing the whispers more clearly. At first I could not make them out, but as months went by they started to become clearer — and sometimes not. Try being in a large room while everyone's talking at the same time. That's the only way I can describe it — kind of like trying to pick out that single voice and grasp it. But then again, there are times that one, two, or sometimes three will be alone, talking to me. It took me years to figure this out. Still, I can never quite stop them — I just deal with them. But all in all, they're kind of like old friends, as I have gotten used to them over time.

I often think back to the yesterday years and try to reflect on the things that could have been different. Life's challenges are hard throughout time. You have to have an anchor that grounds you. But don't let the past define the person you can become — for we all are unique in our own ways that defy who you are. Because in a sense, fate is watching. And whatever you do will always echo through eternity. And God is watching.

Back with Family

Coming Home

From 1975 on up is somewhat of a blur — just bits and pieces mainly. My mom was able to get me back from my grandfather somehow, which I was extremely happy about — but it was also short-lived.

You see, mom was also an alcoholic and drug addict as well. In all reality, because now I had a sister and little brother, I don't think mom quite understood the reality of a family. I mean, I knew I had a sister and brother already. But coming home was extremely different for me, and I quite didn't understand where I fit in.

I often think maybe she felt she had made a mistake. After all, I was a bit wild, to say the least — and that's speaking it mildly. I know I didn't fit well in school and I was always fighting, it seemed. I think she felt I was out of control. I know I saw a lot of doctors and mental health doctors in my first year back with her, and medication was prescribed to me to calm me down. All it did was make me lash out as I fought the meds they put me on.

Alternative placement followed — foster homes and detention facilities for out-of-control juveniles. Fighting, anger, rage, and blackouts — that's all I remember. Wild. I guess you could say I was wild — and treated as such.

"From the last chapter all I remember was depression, loneliness, and a hell of a lot of anger and pain throughout the years — but I had one shining light."

My sister's dad — God rest his soul — taught me how to control my anger somewhat. He was once a Navy SEAL in SEAL Team 7. He was a man that didn't take anything from anyone, but never looked for trouble.

Then one night I awoke to go to the bathroom — Teddy bear in hand. Yes, I slept with a Teddy bear. Anyone got a problem with that? I heard loud crashing noises and people shouting. Half asleep, I opened the bathroom door and saw a man in the hallway, his back toward me. That's when I heard what sounded like an explosion — and what I can only describe as being sprayed — a wetness that covered me from my face, a warm sensation slowly dripping down all of my chin and over my Teddy bear.

My dad had shot a man in the head. The back of that man's head — brains, call it what you will — hit me in the face and all over my Teddy bear. I remember stillness, a quiet calm, as I was trying to grasp what had just happened. But I could not figure it out — as if I was in a bad dream and could not wake, but was awake in a reality I could not comprehend.

All I remember after that was him kneeling down in front of me with a look in his eyes of sorrow for what had just happened. Screaming — screaming in my head — I remember, but nothing coming out. Like I could not talk, nor breathe. Just dead silence. Frozen in time.

I don't think I spoke for maybe a year — maybe longer. And all I remember now is that taste. That taste in my mouth as I reflect on it now. Turns out the man was trying to get with my mom and was caught and tried to kill my dad — my stepfather — but dad blew half his head off, and I got caught in the middle.

Themes of the Journey

Reflections on Luke's Story

🩸 Trauma and Its Lasting Imprint

Luke vividly conveys the deep imprint of traumatic events on his life — his grandmother's death, his grandfather's harshness, the violence he witnessed and endured. The emotional walls his mind built were not weakness — they were survival. Understanding this is the first step toward compassion, for others and for ourselves.

🦅 Identity and the Warrior Heritage

Being of mixed Lakota Sioux and Scandinavian descent reflects a broader struggle many people face in reconciling different aspects of their heritage. Luke's blood carries two warrior traditions — and his life has proven that lineage matters. He was built for the battles he had to face.

🔥 The Cycle of Violence

The narrative illustrates how violence permeates familial relationships across generations. Luke's grandfather's rage, his own anger and fighting — these depict a painful legacy that was hard to escape. Yet Luke's greatest act of strength was deciding to break that cycle.

🌅 Hope Amidst Darkness

Despite the heavy themes, there is a relentless thread of hope woven throughout Luke's story. His desire to use his experiences to help others — to bring brothers and sisters into his coalition — speaks to a powerful commitment to change and healing that no prison, no trauma, and no voice in the night could extinguish.

💬 The Whispers Within

The imagery of whispers in his mind is evocative and deeply human. It symbolizes the inner turmoil and conflict he faced, as well as the long journey toward understanding and accepting his past. Over time, even the voices became — in his own words — "kind of like old friends."

The Coalition

Luke Ferguson doesn't just carry his story for himself. He carries it for every brother and sister who has ever felt lost, locked up, cast aside, or forgotten. His coalition is built on a simple, radical idea: love is the answer. Not love as a soft, easy thing — but love as a warrior's commitment to lifting others up, regardless of color, background, or the sins of the past.

✦
Luke Ferguson is his name. Moving forward with love is his game. Everybody — it doesn't matter what color, regardless of the past — he is here. He wants to bring everybody together at last.

From Warpath to Waymaker

Luke Arrow Ferguson walked a road that would have broken most men. He was born into violence, raised in chaos, lost in systems designed to discard people like him. He earned a name in darkness — Warpath — a name that once stood for danger.

But names can be transformed. Like Saul who became Paul. Like a warrior who lays down his weapons not in defeat, but in the ultimate act of strength — choosing to build instead of destroy, to lift instead of crush, to love instead of hate.

That is who Luke Ferguson is today. And his story — every raw, painful, honest word of it — is his gift to you.
